Below is a simulated content-development exercise for an Individual-Level AI Readiness scale. It follows a standard scale-development logic: define the construct, generate items deductively and inductively, assess content adequacy using judges, refine wording and coverage, and retain a balanced set of items for later empirical testing. Hinkin explicitly recommends combining theoretical definition with inductive item generation, and also recommends a content-adequacy stage in which judges sort items to construct definitions before large-sample testing. I am treating this as a prototype item pool, not a validated instrument. Content-judgment evidence is only one early step in validation; the Standards treat validity as an evidence-based argument rather than something established by a single procedure. Step 1. Item generation1A. Working definition of Individual-Level AI ReadinessIndividual-Level AI Readiness is the degree to which an employee is prepared to understand, evaluate, adopt, use, monitor, and improve AI-enabled tools in a responsible and effective way within their work context.This definition combines:* capability: understanding what AI can and cannot do, * behavior: actually using and improving work with AI, * judgment: checking outputs and escalating risks, * adaptability: learning as tools, policies, and workflows change. 1B. Basic stages in the AI-readiness processA simple process model for individual AI readiness is:1. Understand AI tools, limits, and fit-for-purpose use 2. Experiment with AI in real work tasks 3. Evaluate output quality, bias, and error risk 4. Integrate AI into workflows and team processes 5. Govern responsibly through privacy, security, and escalation 6. Adapt and improve as tools and practices evolve 1C. Deductively defined dimensionsUsing that definition, the construct was divided into six dimensions:1. AI Literacy and Use-Case Understanding 2. AI Experimentation and Task Adoption 3. Critical Evaluation and Risk Judgment 4. Responsible Data and Governance Behavior 5. Workflow Integration and Human-AI Collaboration 6. Continuous Learning and Adaptability 1D. Simulated SME focus groupA simulated focus group was created with managers from:* a large financial-services firm, * a global technology company, * a public-sector employer, * a healthcare operations group, * a manufacturing SME, * a small marketing agency. They were instructed to describe observable employee behaviors that signal AI readiness in their organizations. This matches the inductive approach Hinkin describes, where experts generate examples that are then grouped into themes. 1E. Simulated behavioral statements, then Q-sort themesAfter idea generation, simulated SMEs used a Q-sort style exercise to group statements by similarity to the six construct definitions. That kind of pretesting aligns with the content-adequacy logic described by Hinkin and with the broader testing standards emphasis on evidence from test content. Behavioral statements by themeDimension 1. AI Literacy and Use-Case UnderstandingTheme: Understands what AI does, where it helps, and where it should not be used.* L1. Explains in simple terms what an AI tool is doing and what kind of output it produces. * L2. Distinguishes tasks that are appropriate for AI support from tasks that still require full human judgment. * L3. Identifies when an AI tool is being used for prediction, generation, summarization, or classification. * L4. Describes common limitations of AI tools, such as hallucinations, incomplete context, or outdated knowledge. * L5. Chooses AI tools based on job requirements rather than novelty. * L6. Recognizes that different AI tools are better suited to different work problems. Dimension 2. AI Experimentation and Task AdoptionTheme: Tries AI in work tasks and translates it into practical productivity gains.* E1. Tests AI tools on small, low-risk work tasks before wider use. * E2. Uses AI to generate first drafts, options, or summaries that speed up work. * E3. Revises prompts or inputs when the first output is weak. * E4. Compares AI-assisted and non-AI approaches to decide which is more efficient. * E5. Volunteers to pilot new AI-enabled workflows when relevant to the job. * E6. Looks for repeated tasks that could be partially supported by AI. Dimension 3. Critical Evaluation and Risk JudgmentTheme: Reviews AI outputs carefully and knows when not to trust them.* C1. Checks AI outputs for factual accuracy before using them. * C2. Notices when an AI answer sounds confident but is unsupported. * C3. Seeks corroborating evidence before using AI output in important decisions. * C4. Recognizes when an AI-generated recommendation may be biased or incomplete. * C5. Refuses to rely on AI output when the stakes are too high for unverified use. * C6. Adjusts reliance on AI based on the risk, complexity, and consequences of the task. Dimension 4. Responsible Data and Governance BehaviorTheme: Uses AI in ways that respect privacy, policy, and organizational controls.* G1. Avoids entering confidential, regulated, or personally identifiable data into unapproved AI tools. * G2. Follows organizational rules for approved AI systems and acceptable uses. * G3. Knows when an AI use case requires legal, compliance, or security review. * G4. Flags risky AI use cases involving sensitive decisions or protected information. * G5. Documents how AI was used when transparency is required. * G6. Understands that convenience does not justify bypassing AI governance rules. Dimension 5. Workflow Integration and Human-AI CollaborationTheme: Fits AI into real workflows while maintaining human accountability.* W1. Uses AI outputs as inputs to work rather than as unquestioned final products. * W2. Builds review steps so that human judgment remains accountable for final decisions. * W3. Shares useful prompting or workflow practices with coworkers. * W4. Adapts team processes when AI can improve turnaround time or consistency. * W5. Clarifies which parts of a task should be automated, augmented, or kept fully human. * W6. Coordinates with others so AI-supported work is consistent across the team. Dimension 6. Continuous Learning and AdaptabilityTheme: Keeps learning as AI tools, risks, and work expectations change.* A1. Seeks feedback on how effectively they use AI in their role. * A2. Updates their practices when AI tools or policies change. * A3. Learns from failed AI attempts instead of abandoning the tool immediately. * A4. Invests time in building better prompting, checking, and review habits. * A5. Stays informed about new AI capabilities relevant to their job. * A6. Adjusts expectations as they learn more about what AI can and cannot do. Step 2. Item reduction through simulated judge sortingA panel of 10 simulated judges was created and asked to assign each item to the one dimension it best represented. This reflects the content-adequacy stage described in scale-development work: judges classify items against construct definitions, and weakly sorted items are candidates for removal or revision. Retention rule: keep items with 80% or greater agreement.Initial content-adequacy resultsDimension 1. Item refinement and bandwidth assessmentA second round of simulated focus-group review was used to identify:* redundant items, * vague wording, * domain gaps where the inductive items were narrower than the deductive construct. This step was used to improve construct bandwidth, so the final pool covered all major parts of AI readiness instead of over-weighting only tool use or only caution.Simulated focus-group refinementsMain changes:* Replaced vague Ňtool enthusiasmÓ language with more observable work behavior. * Strengthened the distinction between using AI, evaluating AI, and governing AI. * Added items to ensure the construct was not overly productivity-focused. * Tightened wording so each item reflected one main idea, consistent with standard item-writing guidance. New or revised items added for bandwidthTo ensure at least five strong items per dimension, the following refined items were introduced:* L7. Explains why a given work task is or is not suitable for AI assistance. * E7. Uses trial-and-error to improve AI-supported task performance while staying within approved boundaries. * C7. Distinguishes between outputs that are acceptable for low-risk drafting and outputs that require rigorous verification. * G7. Stops using an AI tool for a task when policy, privacy, or regulatory concerns become unclear. * W7. Incorporates AI into a workflow in ways that make handoffs, approvals, and accountability clear. * A7. Regularly updates their AI work habits based on feedback, new guidance, or new tool capabilities. Final simulated judge sortA final panel of 10 simulated judges sorted the revised item set. All retained final items met the 80% agreement rule.Final retained item agreement percentages:* L1 100%, L2 90%, L4 90%, L6 80%, L7 90% * E1 90%, E2 80%, E4 80%, E6 90%, E7 90% * C1 100%, C2 90%, C3 80%, C4 90%, C7 90% * G1 100%, G2 90%, G3 80%, G6 90%, G7 80% * W1 90%, W2 100%, W3 80%, W5 90%, W7 90% * A1 80%, A2 90%, A3 80%, A5 80%, A7 90% Step 4.
